1. Z-Score Overview

Z-Score is a statistical standardization method used to quantify how far a data point deviates from its historical average, measured in units of standard deviation. In this system, Z-Score is applied to Bitcoin price data to normalize raw price movements and make them comparable across time.

Raw Bitcoin prices are highly volatile and scale-dependent, which makes it difficult to directly identify meaningful patterns. Z-Score transforms this raw data into a standardized form, allowing the system to distinguish between normal market fluctuations and statistically abnormal price behavior.

  • Œº ‚Äî Mean price over the rolling lookback window
  • œÉ ‚Äî Standard deviation over the same window

By converting raw price data into Z-Score values, the system effectively filters out insignificant noise and highlights price movements that deviate meaningfully from historical norms. These deviations form the statistical foundation for later strategy evaluation.

üìà Interpreting Z-Score Extremes

Z-Score is primarily used to identify statistically abnormal price behavior. When the absolute value of the Z-Score becomes large, it indicates that the current price has deviated significantly from its historical average and is no longer behaving within a normal range.

  • High positive Z-Score indicates that the price is far above its historical mean, suggesting a potential state of price extension.
  • Low negative Z-Score indicates that the price is far below its historical mean, suggesting a potential state of price compression.

These extreme deviations do not directly imply an immediate trading action. Instead, they serve as contextual signals that are later evaluated by the selected strategy logic, entry thresholds, exit thresholds, and execution rules defined in the Strategy Parameters section.

By separating statistical detection from execution logic, the system ensures that Z-Score is used purely as a feature extraction and normalization mechanism, while trading decisions remain fully configurable and strategy-dependent.

2. Strategy Parameters

üéØ Strategy Type

Two execution modes determine how trades are triggered:

  • Trend Strategy
    • Z-Score > Entry ‚Üí open/hold long
    • Z-Score < Exit ‚Üí open/hold short
  • Fast Strategy
    • Z-Score > Entry ‚Üí open long
    • Z-Score < Entry ‚Üí close long
    • Z-Score < Exit ‚Üí open short
    • Z-Score > Exit ‚Üí close short

üìè Z-Score Window Size

Rolling lookback window used to calculate the Z-Score of Bitcoin prices. A larger window produces smoother signals, while a smaller window reacts faster but may be noisier. Typical values range from 50‚Äì600 candles.

üìà Entry Threshold

Z-Score level that triggers a new position. Higher thresholds reduce trade frequency and focus on stronger dislocations; lower thresholds create more signals.

üìâ Exit Threshold

Z-Score level used to close or reverse positions. When Z-Score moves back toward the mean, the strategy exits or switches direction. Usually closer to zero than the entry.

üîÅ Trading Side

Defines which position directions are allowed during the backtest:

  • Long only: Opens buy positions only.
  • Short only: Opens sell positions only.
  • Both: Strategy can switch between long and short.

3. Performance Metrics

üìä Sharpe Ratio

Measures risk-adjusted returns. Higher Sharpe values indicate better performance per unit of volatility.

‚ö° Calmar Ratio

Compares annual return to maximum drawdown. High values represent strong performance with low downside risk.

üìâ Max Drawdown

Largest decline from peak to trough during the backtest. Represents worst-case scenario loss.

üí∞ Annualized Return

Estimated yearly growth rate based on compounding. Useful for comparing strategies across different time periods.

üíµ Total Return

üîÑ Number of Trades
